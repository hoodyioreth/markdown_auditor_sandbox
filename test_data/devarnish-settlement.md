Devarnish
=========

Devarnish is not merely a rich Velunan city. It is a place where roads are made to slow, gates are made to count, and trade is made to yield profit before it passes on. Built at the eastern hinge of the kingdom, Devarnish stands where movement narrows through ridge and stream into something measurable. Caravans are checked here. Bargains are made here. Goods are taxed, warehoused, refined, and resold here. And because so much wealth passes beneath its walls, every power in the city has learned the same dangerous lesson: whoever governs Devarnish does not simply govern a city, but touches the purse-strings of a kingdom.  

> **Devarnish** is the great eastern trade city of Veluna and the third largest urban center in the kingdom after Veluna City and Mitrik. Set in a natural bottleneck cut by the **Mill Stream** through **Hog Back Ridge**, it commands the flow of eastern commerce entering the realm. Three ornate gates regulate the main approaches, while the city markets, warehouses, workshops, and guild compounds turn raw material into finished wealth. This is not merely a city where trade passes through. It is a city that transforms trade into power.
> 
>  
> 
> For the Dungeon Master, Devarnish is one of the best Velunan cities for active play because it joins together customs law, guild rivalry, episcopal reform, noble authority, and regional diplomacy. The current Count, **Westin Mori**, has spent six difficult years trying to reclaim secular control from interests that prospered under his father’s indifference. **Bishop Marquin Dorasie** treats the city as a moral battlefield. The guildmasters have fortunes vast enough to resist them both. And with northern east-west routes increasingly squeezed by Iuz, more trade than ever now moves through Devarnish and along the river routes tied to Jarl, making every policy dispute in the city matter beyond its walls.

\---

### What Devarnish Is

Devarnish is the city where eastern trade becomes Velunan wealth. That is the plainest truth about it, and every other truth in the city grows from there. It is not simply an old diocesan seat with a marketplace attached. It is a city built upon flow — of wagons, pack trains, barges, freight, tolls, invoices, contracts, labor, and ambition. The men who govern it know that. The priests who condemn it know that. The guildmasters who enrich themselves there know it best of all.

 

The city’s importance comes from position as much as from size. It stands where land movement narrows into a controllable urban throat, and because of that, much of the commerce entering Veluna from the east must either pass through Devarnish itself or be reckoned against it. That makes the city unusually legible to a Dungeon Master: every gate matters, every road choice matters, every warehouse record matters, and every dispute over customs or convoys can become a political question with diocesan and even kingdom-wide consequence.

* * *

#### The Shape of the City — Mill Stream and Hog Back Ridge

The source material places Devarnish in a natural bottleneck cut by the **Mill Stream** through **Hog Back Ridge** on its way toward the Velverdyva. This is the city’s defining physical fact. Devarnish is not sprawled across open country like a loose market town. It is pinched into significance by terrain. Its walls, streets, gates, and market controls all become more plausible and more powerful because the land itself favors concentration and supervision.

 

For the DM, this means the city should feel **compressed, channeled, and watchful**. Approaches are not infinite. Traffic does not simply pour in from all directions without scrutiny. Ridge, watercourse, and road law together force movement into lanes that can be observed and monetized. This physical structure supports everything else about the city: taxes at the gates, guarded market access, visible noble power, and guild competition for the right to stand nearest the streams of wealth.

 

A good table image of Devarnish is this: stone walls and controlled entrances, roofs crowded close where the ground narrows, market streets thick with labor and noise, bridges and culverts over the Mill Stream, the smell of wet timber, carts, wool, forge smoke, wax, and grain, and above it all the sense that every man entering the city is entering a place already prepared to profit from him.

* * *

#### The Three Gates and the Price of Entry

Devarnish’s **three ornate city gates** are among the most important practical institutions in the city. They are not merely decorative civic works. They are instruments of revenue and control. The sources make plain that the Count uses them to monitor trade moving along the **Great Western Road** and the **Iron Road**, and that goods and materials are subject to taxation as they pass. The city therefore lives by a principle few travelers love but all must learn: nothing of consequence enters Devarnish for free. :contentReference\[oaicite:5\]{index=5} :contentReference\[oaicite:6\]{index=6}

 

Each gate should be treated by the DM as a different kind of encounter space. One may feel more heavily commercial, thick with wagons, drovers, customs inspections, and shouting clerks. Another may be more military or civic, with officers, writ-checking, and controlled passage for important persons. A third may feel more tied to local traffic and labor movement. The exact sub-article layout can be written later, but the city article should already establish that the gates are where Devarnish turns geography into law.

 

**At the table, the gates are useful for:**

*   Customs inspections and confiscations
*   Bribery attempts and false manifests
*   Arrival scenes that immediately establish civic pressure
*   Warrants, embargoes, quarantines, and emergency closures
*   Noble or episcopal processions that show who controls public spectacle

* * *

#### The Veluna Markets — Where the Realm Buys the East

Within the gates lie the markets that make Devarnish famous across Veluna. Here one finds trade from across the Flanaess, not merely passing hands in raw form, but being graded, combined, processed, and transformed. The source material is especially useful on this point: raw material from many places is often brought together here and worked by master craftsmen into goods of higher worth. That means Devarnish is not just a trade city but a **value-making city**. Timber becomes cabinetry. Iron becomes finished goods. Cloth is dyed, cut, and resold. Leather is fitted, stamped, and bound. Bulk goods become refined ones. :contentReference\[oaicite:7\]{index=7}

 

This helps explain why the guilds have become so powerful. A city that simply passes wagons onward can be rich. A city that captures the labor and finishing stages of commerce becomes politically dangerous. Every bale of wool, every crate of metal stock, every wagon of hides or oil or wax represents not only tax revenue, but workshop profit, apprenticeship labor, guild contracts, and the expansion of private patronage. Thus the markets of Devarnish are not merely noisy and colorful; they are the visible face of the city’s hidden power structure.

 

**The markets should feel like:**

*   A forest of stalls, sheds, arcades, counting tables, and workshop fronts
*   An environment of inspectors, guild apprentices, drovers, porters, scribes, and petty officials
*   A place where foreign wares can be found, but where local finishing work is prized above raw import alone
*   A place where nearly every deal has a witness, a seal, a tax, or a rival listening nearby

#### The Workshop City and the Power of the Guilds

Devarnish’s true political complexity begins with its workshops. The source material notes plainly that the concentration of craftsmen in the city has created powerful guilds that control the workshops. This should not be read as background flavor. It is the central civic fact beneath the civic fact of trade. Workshops mean labor. Labor means loyalty. Loyalty means power. A guild that can close work yards, delay high-value finishing, reroute contracts, or squeeze warehouse release dates can hurt a count more quietly than many soldiers could. :contentReference\[oaicite:8\]{index=8}

 

For that reason, Devarnish is a city where noble and clerical authority must reckon with organized mercantile power every day. The guilds are not all equal, nor do they all speak with one voice, but together they form a class of entrenched interest that thrived under Count Westin’s father and has no desire to return meekly to a more disciplined order.

 

**A DM should think of the guilds as controlling:**

*   Workshop districts and specialist labor
*   Access to master craftsmen and apprentices
*   Fulfillment schedules for high-value goods
*   Much of the informal credit and recommendation network used by merchants
*   A hidden political conversation running beneath the public one of count and bishop

#### Count Westin Mori’s Devarnish

Count **Westin Mori** has ruled for six years, and his city has resisted him for nearly all of them. He is young, forceful, Suloise, and aligned with **St. Cuthbert**, which gives his rule a harder and more openly disciplinary tone than that of his father. Westin believes his father surrendered too much of the city to guildmasters and gave Bishop Marquin too much practical latitude. Since taking office he has worked to undo that softness through law, funding reversals, and a stronger secular hand in nearly every matter that shapes urban life.

 

In Devarnish City specifically, this means Westin is trying to reclaim the city’s public machinery: gate policy, civic revenue, legal oversight, market regulation, and the symbolic fact that the Count must once more be seen as the first ruler of the city rather than its ceremonial landlord. This puts him in conflict with nearly everyone who grew wealthy or comfortable under the old arrangement. Devarnish under Westin is therefore a city where even routine administrative questions can become ideological ones.

 

**When using Westin in city play, remember:**

*   He is reformist, but not gentle
*   He is serious about reclaiming rule, not merely posturing about it
*   He sees the guilds as overgrown and the bishop as overindulged
*   He is isolated enough that every reliable ally matters greatly

* * *

#### Bishop Marquin’s Devarnish

Bishop **Marquin Dorasie** of [The Order of Rao](the-order-of-rao-organization.md) sees the city very differently. To him, Devarnish is not chiefly the gateway of trade but the moral wound of Veluna. For twenty years he has waged a campaign against what he perceives as entrenched corruption, vice, softness, luxury, and civic compromise. Under the old count, he enjoyed wide freedom to pursue these efforts. Under Westin, he has been checked, frustrated, and publicly contradicted.

 

This means the Bishop’s Devarnish is a city of hidden dossiers, stern sermons, moral scrutiny, reform projects, and ambitions toward firmer religious order. His proposal to establish a body of **Raoan Knights** in the city is especially important, because it would create a visible institutional arm for his campaign against disorder. Whether those knights would protect the city’s soul or simply deepen the contest for urban authority depends greatly on who is telling the tale.

 

**When using Marquin in city play, remember:**

*   He is not a passive priest but a long-standing urban power
*   He believes the city is spiritually diseased and must be corrected
*   He resents the shrinking of the freedom he once enjoyed
*   He is likely to frame even practical policy disputes as moral ones

* * *

#### A City of Public Confrontation

The defining civic drama of Devarnish in 576 CY is that the Count and the Bishop no longer veil their struggle. Their disputes have become public facts. This shapes the city’s atmosphere. Market inspectors wonder whose regulations will prevail. Clerks weigh whether a given order is lawful, pious, or both. Guildmasters decide which side to flatter and which to quietly undermine. Even ordinary citizens begin to sort their loyalties through the language of order, corruption, reform, privilege, and rightful rule.

 

**The main questions beneath the quarrel are these:**

*   **Who truly governs the city?** The Count says noble secular authority. The Bishop says moral authority must prevail where wealth has corrupted ordinary rule.
*   **Who disciplines urban vice?** Westin wants law, order, and accountable civic power. Marquin wants a deeper and more relentless cleansing.
*   **Who benefits from Devarnish’s wealth?** The Count wants revenue and obedience. The Bishop wants reform. The guilds want profit and leverage.

 

This makes Devarnish a superb city for campaigns that depend on visible politics rather than hidden court intrigue alone. Here, power walks in daylight, argues in public, and then pays men in private to shape what follows.

* * *

#### Guildmaster Markei Poston — The High Roads Magnate

Of all the non-noble powers in the city, **Guildmaster Markei Poston** is the most imposing. A half-elven master of the **High Roads Trading Company**, he commands the largest trading concern in Veluna and possesses contacts stretching across much of the Flanaess. He is rich enough to live as though civic dignity were a costume nobles happened to wear first. His eastern mansion compound rivals the Count’s own establishment and serves as a silent declaration that trade has made a new aristocracy whether heralds approve or not.

 

Markei is especially dangerous because he does not need to seize formal office to weaken it. He can delay contracts, redirect freight, tighten credit, reward friendly officials, and place pressure on any ruler whose reforms threaten his interests. Westin Mori has sought a way to bring him back beneath the city’s proper order and has not yet succeeded.

* * *

#### Guildmaster Lorit Baskin — The Quiet Counterweight

**Lorit Baskin**, master of the **Iron Rings Consortium**, is Markei’s chief rival and one of Count Westin’s few practical allies within the commercial sphere. Yet he is no simple friend of law. Lorit is seldom seen in public, acting through employees and intermediaries whenever possible. This gives him a colder, more deliberate cast than his brighter rival. Rumor links him to the old count’s death, though nothing was proved. In Devarnish, such rumors do not cling to harmless men.

 

Lorit is valuable to a DM because he embodies a different kind of guild power: not flamboyant mercantile grandeur, but silent reach. He is the kind of urban magnate who may help a ruler defeat one rival only to become the next problem waiting in the dark behind the ledger.

* * *

#### Eastbound Traffic — Jarl or the Iron Road

Most traffic leaving Devarnish for the east now prefers **Jarl** and the **Velverdyva route**. The city’s merchants and factors understand risk well enough to prefer barges, wharves, and accounted cargo over the deteriorating uncertainties of the woodland road. This makes Jarl the practical eastern limb of Devarnish’s trade system, even though the capital itself remains the dominant customs and market seat.

 

The **Iron Road**, while still direct in principle, has declined in reliability as it enters the Iron Woods. Travelers know of fallen trees, washed-out stretches, and the general sense that the farther one goes, the less the city’s peace can be trusted to accompany him. Because of this, the choice between river and road becomes one of the city’s most useful recurring practical questions. Every caravan master, courier, priest, investigator, and smuggler must decide which form of danger he prefers.

 

**For the city article, this matters because:**

*   The wealth of Devarnish is partly shaped by traffic being steered away from riskier woodland travel
*   Jarl’s growth strengthens the city’s downstream political and logistical reach
*   The Iron Road remains important precisely because it is dangerous and direct
*   Players can feel the city’s strategic role every time they must choose how to leave it

* * *

#### The City in the Wider Campaign

Devarnish is one of the first Velunan cities likely to react when eastern events become too large to ignore. If trade confidence begins to fail in Verbobonc, Devarnish notices quickly. If a diplomatic scandal threatens the routes to Celene or the standing of Veluna abroad, Devarnish notices quickly. If inquiries must be assembled, records searched, caravans diverted, or escorted travel prepared, Devarnish has the officials, guilds, and transport networks to do it. In campaign terms, it is a **response city**.

 

This is especially important for the broader **Gift of Beauty** thread and the missing clergy associated with it. Devarnish is exactly the kind of city where a Velunan investigation would gather freight records, river schedules, escort contracts, witness statements, and clerical resolve before looking eastward. The city does not merely hear bad news. It prepares the wagons and barges that answer it.

#### What the Temple of Elemental Evil Would Exploit Here

An enemy need not storm Devarnish to harm Veluna through it. A clever foe would aim instead at the city’s confidence. Compromise the toll books. Bribe a customs officer. Turn one guild against another. Make the Count think the Bishop shelters sedition. Make the Bishop think the Count protects vice. Spread fear on the Iron Road, uncertainty on the river, and suspicion through the markets. The city’s greatness depends upon trusted movement, and trusted movement is always easier to spoil than to build.

 

**The likeliest urban seams to attack are these:**

*   **Gate records and customs seals**
*   **Guild rivalry and contract sabotage**
*   **River manifests and warehouse inventory fraud**
*   **Public scandal between Count and Bishop**
*   **Fear campaigns tied to the Iron Road or eastern woodland travel**

#### DM Notes: Using Devarnish at the Table

**Use Devarnish when thou wantest a city where movement and politics are one and the same.**

*   **For civic pressure:** customs inspections, toll disputes, entry restrictions, cargo seizures
*   **For market intrigue:** guild sabotage, workshop contracts, counterfeit goods, cargo switching, master-craftsman rivalries
*   **For noble and clerical conflict:** public hearings, reform edicts, funding disputes, questions of jurisdiction
*   **For travel setup:** choosing between Jarl and the Velverdyva or the Iron Road through the woods
*   **For regional consequences:** any event in the east that touches trade, diplomacy, or safe movement should echo here fast

 

**DM shorthand:** “_Devarnish is where Veluna turns the east into money, and where money keeps trying to become government._”

##### WHY DEVARNISH MATTERS FOR YOUR CAMPAIGN

Devarnish is one of the strongest Velunan city articles for active campaign use because it binds together several major engines at once:

 

*   **Trade:** gates, tolls, markets, workshops, guild compounds, road and river decisions
*   **Politics:** Count Westin Mori and Bishop Marquin Dorasie are in visible struggle over who rules the city
*   **Guild Power:** Markei Poston and Lorit Baskin make commerce into a rival form of authority
*   **Travel Play:** Devarnish is the last major reckoning point before travelers choose Jarl or the Iron Road
*   **Regional Relevance:** eastern news, trade disruption, and diplomatic consequences strike here early and loudly

 

Use Devarnish when thou needest a city that feels watched, taxed, useful, proud, and perpetually on the edge of argument. This is where Veluna’s commerce, conscience, and authority collide in public — and where adventurers may find that every road east begins with someone in Devarnish wanting a say in why they travel it.
