F7 Barloon’s Stoneworks
=======================

> This cozy, low-ceiling room feels grand due to the intricate tiling on the floor and the delicate stonework of the ceiling and walls. The patterns are geometric and abstract but harmonious. You think you can see beasts or figures depicted in them, but it’s like picking shapes out of clouds.

Barloon’s Stoneworks is a soot-stained, echoing masonry yard and carving hall that supplies dressed stone, funerary markers, road blocks, and architectural ornamentation throughout Verbobonc. Situated in the Foreign Quarter near the river roads and warehouses, the yard never truly rests—wagons arrive at dawn, chisels ring through the afternoon, and finished blocks are carted away under torchlight.

To most, it is simply a hardworking guildholding. To those who look closer, it is also a perfect cover—a place where heavy materials move freely, laborers change often, and few ask questions so long as the stone is true and the coin is good.

##### Barloon’s Stoneworks Approach

The nearer you draw, the more the street changes. Cobblestones give way to grit and pale dust, and the air tastes of stone—dry, sharp, and honest. A yard opens behind a low wall of stacked blocks, each slab marked in chalk with numbers, angles, and strange dwarven shorthand.  
  
The sound of hammer on chisel rings out in clean, measured strokes, as if the very rhythm were a prayer. Within the cutting shed, lamplight gleams on fresh-cut faces of granite and limestone. Dust hangs in the beams like fog caught in sunlight. A heavy anvil sits near the rear wall—not for show, but for work—and above the lintel of an inner doorway you spy a small carving: hammer and anvil, worn smooth by years and reverent fingers.  
  
A dwarf steps into view, broad as a gatepost, beard bound in a craftsman’s braid. He does not smile, yet neither does he scowl. He studies you the way a mason studies a block—judging cracks, weight, and worth. At last he speaks, voice like gravel poured into a bowl. _“If thou’rt here to waste breath, take it elsewhere. If thou’rt here to build… then say what stone thou need’st, and why.”_

The stoneworks consists of an open yard piled with rough blocks, a long cutting shed open to the street, and a rear storage hall where finished pieces are stacked beneath tarps. Cranes creak overhead, and stone dust coats everything—clothes, lungs, and tempers alike.

Barloon runs a tight operation. Wages are fair, contracts honored, and discipline enforced with a barked word or a withheld payment rather than violence. That reputation keeps the city’s builders loyal—and keeps inspectors from lingering too long.

##### **Atmosphere**

*   Constant hammering and grinding
*   Smell of wet stone, dust, and oil
*   Workers shout measurements across the yard
*   Visitors are watched, not greeted
*   No idle conversation unless earned

Stonecutters here respect **competence** above all else. Anyone who looks soft is ignored. Anyone who looks useful is tested.

### History — A Legacy of Stone and Survival

The Barloon brothers trace their lineage to the **Lortmil Mountains**, where their ancestors once dwelt in the lost dwarven city of **Ulthak-Nor**, destroyed during the chaos of the **Hateful Wars**. When the wars swept the mountains clear of humanoid tribes, they also shattered many dwarven holds, scattering clans into exile.

In the aftermath, the Barloon line endured a long period of displacement before helping to establish **Irondelve**, a reborn dwarven mining town carved into a northern Lortmil peak after rich veins of ore were rediscovered. From these mountains still come the stone the brothers prize above all others.

In the years following the wars, Balin, Korin, and Dorin Barloon migrated to **Verbobonc**, bringing with them both Lortmil stone and the old ways of dwarven craft. There they founded **Barloon’s Stoneworks**, reviving ancestral techniques of carving, balance, and load-bearing design long absent from human construction.

Their work—precise, enduring, and unmistakably dwarven—soon attracted the attention of merchants and nobles alike. Today, Barloon’s Stoneworks stands as both a thriving business and a quiet testament to dwarven perseverance: proof that though cities may fall, stone remembers those who shape it well.

### Location and Services

_Crafting Stone into Art_

The brothers maintain noble patronage with clients like Lady Kathryn Sarcina and Lord Haxx, merchant connections with Lakash and the Stonemasons’ Guild, and strong ties with the dwarven community in the Town of Irondelve and the Lortmil Mountains. Barloon’s Stoneworks features a cozy showroom with intricate tiling and a large stone yard with stone blocks in various stages of completion. Services include stone imports and sales, custom carvings, architectural stonework, and sculptures, with prices ranging from 5 gp to 500 gp.

*   **Cozy Low-Ceiling Room:** A cozy showroom with intricate tiling and delicate stonework.
*   **Large Stone Yard:** Filled with stone blocks in various stages of completion, from rough-hewn to finely carved.

### Relationships

_Connecting with Nobles, Merchants, and Fellow Dwarves_

Balin Barloon, the eldest brother and master stonemason, oversees the stoneworks with a stern and meticulous eye. Korin Barloon, the middle brother and business manager, handles stone imports and negotiations, while Dorin Barloon, the youngest brother, is an eager apprentice learning the craft.

#### **DM Notes**

*   Barloon is not evil, corrupt, or secretly aligned—he is **pragmatic**.
*   Pressing him hard earns nothing; showing respect for craft earns conversation.
*   The yard is loud—perfect for whispered deals that vanish into the noise.
*   Stone remembers where it came from. Tracking quarries can matter later.

**Associated Dwarven Orders:**

*   Hammers of Moradin — spiritual guardians of dwarven tradition and craftsmanship

**Secondary Observances (Cultural, not Devotional):**

Respect for Beory as the World Mother is acknowledged academically by the brothers due to their work in surface stone and earth, but not worshiped.

*   St. Cuthbert (law, contracts, honest labor)
*   Old Faith of Beory (stone, earth, endurance; quiet personal veneration among workers)
