Diocese of Devarnish
====================

Devarnish is the easternmost diocese of Veluna — the place where the kingdom’s order meets its uncertainty. It is a land of gates, roads, barges, ledgers, warehouses, tolls, and uneasy authority, where trade from east and west is measured beneath the eyes of both bishop and count. Here Veluna does not appear chiefly as a land of contemplation, but as a realm of movement: river traffic on the **Velverdyva**, caravans rolling the **Great Western Road**, and merchants deciding every day whether they trust the **Iron Road** through the woods or would rather make for **Jarl** and the safer river route. In 576 CY, Devarnish matters more than ever, because the disorder of the wider Flanaess is driving more wealth, more travelers, and more risk directly into its hands.  

> **The Diocese of Devarnish** is the easternmost and one of the most important dioceses of Veluna — a wealthy border march whose roads, rivers, towns, and markets make it the kingdom’s chief eastern trade gateway. Its capital, **Devarnish City**, is the third largest city in Veluna and sits on the western side of the diocese, guarding the great choke point through which eastern commerce enters the realm. Its other key settlement, **Jarl**, is a large river trade town on the Velverdyva where traffic from road and water alike is watched, taxed, warehoused, and judged.
> 
>  
> 
> For Dungeon Masters, Devarnish is one of the most useful dioceses in the Veluna sandbox because it brings together nearly every force that matters: noble authority, Raoan discipline, guild wealth, river commerce, border anxiety, and eastward diplomacy. The Count and the Bishop are openly at odds. The guildmasters are rich enough to defy them both. The Iron Road grows more dangerous the farther it enters the woods. And with northern east-west traffic choked by Iuz, ever more merchants now choose the Great Western Road and the Velverdyva, making Devarnish richer, tenser, and more politically volatile by the year.

 

### What the Diocese of Devarnish Is

The Diocese of Devarnish is the eastern threshold of Veluna — not merely a prosperous district, but the place where the kingdom receives much of the wider world. It is a trade diocese above all else, rich in movement, markets, customs, tolls, and secondary workmanship. If Mitrik is Veluna’s sacred heart, and Veluna City its political and mercantile center, then Devarnish is the realm’s **eastern counting-house**, where routes are judged, goods are taxed, and foreign traffic is translated into Velunan profit.

 

Yet Devarnish is not simply rich. It is _exposed_. Its place at the edge of the kingdom means that rumor, danger, and opportunity arrive here before they settle deeper into the realm. Roads from the Viscounty and farther eastern lands approach this diocese first. River traffic from the north and east converges here. Caravans deciding whether to risk the woods or trust the water all shape the life of the diocese. For that reason, Devarnish is not a quiet place. It is a place where wealth forces choices, and those choices have been making enemies for years.

* * *

#### Borders and Geographic Character

Devarnish is the easternmost point of Veluna, and its frontiers are not all equally clear. Its **northern border** is the **Velverdyva River**, broad, busy, and vital to both trade and communication. Its **eastern border** is not a simple line upon a map, but something buried somewhere deep in the **Iron Woods**, where the settled authority of Veluna becomes uncertain and the confidence of road law begins to thin. Its **southern border** is vaguer still, effectively ending where the patrols in the **Kron Hills** cease to maintain real presence. This makes the diocese feel like a region where law is strongest around roads, markets, and river traffic, and weakest where woodland and hill country swallow distance and certainty.

 

This uncertain geography is part of what makes the diocese so useful in play. Devarnish is neither a soft heartland nor a full frontier. It is a **managed edge**: civilized, wealthy, and well-administered in its centers, yet frayed toward its outer margins. A DM should treat its map accordingly. Its strongest truth is not that every border is fixed, but that every important thing in the diocese happens near the routes people can actually trust.

For the Dungeon Master, these demographics matter because they explain why Devarnish feels socially broader, commercially sharper, and politically less uniform than a quieter inland diocese. It is easier for foreign ideas, unusual professions, and mixed loyalties to take root here — and easier as well for bishops, counts, and guilds to disagree over what kind of order ought to rule such a place.

#### Devarnish City — Capital on the Western Edge

The capital, **Devarnish City**, lies on the western side of the diocese rather than at its center. That fact is important. Devarnish does not dominate the eastern woods by proximity. It governs the diocese by controlling the great roads and by receiving trade before it is distributed onward. The city is the third largest in Veluna and serves as the chief customs and market center for eastern traffic. Its three ornate gates, its controlled markets, and the bottleneck terrain around the city make it the place where secular power, guild wealth, and diocesan administration are concentrated most visibly. The source material is clear that the gates and markets are strictly controlled and taxed, and that Devarnish’s workshops turn raw material from many lands into high-value goods. That is why the guilds here are so powerful: Devarnish does not merely pass commerce onward — it improves it, profits from it, and binds craftsmen to its local magnates. :contentReference\[oaicite:4\]{index=4} :contentReference\[oaicite:5\]{index=5}

 

Because the capital lies to the west, most travelers heading east do not linger long once their business is done. They either turn toward **Jarl** and the river route, or they gamble on the **Iron Road**. This gives Devarnish City a distinctive character. It is not the end of a road, but the **last major civilized reckoning point** before men decide how much risk they are willing to bear beyond it.

* * *

#### The Two Eastern Routes — Jarl or the Iron Road

Most traffic heading east from Devarnish chooses **Jarl** and the **Velverdyva route**. That choice alone tells a Dungeon Master a great deal about the region. River trade is safer, more predictable, and more heavily trafficked than the direct woodland crossing. Barges, river craft, and convoyed freight provide a practical alternative to long exposure in the Iron Woods, and merchants are rarely sentimental where risk is concerned. If a shipment can be floated, escorted, and insured more safely by river, most traders will do exactly that.

 

The **Iron Road**, by contrast, does indeed lead east through the Iron Woods toward Verbobonc, but the road’s condition worsens sharply after the woods are reached. Upkeep has been allowed to decline. Fallen trees, washed-out stretches, and the general threat of the deep woods make the route difficult and feared. The only notable haven along it is the **Fallen Timber Tavern**, widely regarded as the one true safe refuge at the road’s midpoint. Travelers who choose the Iron Road do so with urgency, desperation, local knowledge, or poor luck — and many measure their day not by distance gained, but by whether they can reach the tavern before nightfall. This makes the road ideal for adventure use: it is direct enough to matter, dangerous enough to be memorable, and civilized enough to contain one hard point of human defiance in the dark.

 

**For the DM, these two routes create immediate gameplay contrast:**

 

*   **The Jarl / Velverdyva route** favors barges, toll houses, dockside intrigue, customs records, warehouses, and river intelligence
*   **The Iron Road** favors escorts, ambushes, woods travel, damaged wagons, missing couriers, and grim refuge at the Fallen Timber Tavern

#### The Traffic Economy of the Diocese

The economy of Devarnish revolves around **trade and traffic through its cities**. That is the simplest and truest way to understand it. Goods move in, goods are counted, goods are taxed, goods are refined or repacked, and goods move out again. It is an economy of corridors, not isolated estates. The city and town life of the diocese rests on this constant passage: innkeepers, teamsters, chandlers, river pilots, customs clerks, warehouse owners, toll men, drovers, brokers, factors, master craftsmen, bargemen, scribes, and guild apprentices all feed upon the same flow.

 

In the current campaign era this traffic has increased in importance. With **Iuz choking off much of the northern east-west traffic**, more and more caravans and merchants have turned to the **Great Western Road** and the **Velverdyva** to move east and west. That means Devarnish is not merely thriving by old habit. It is becoming more strategically important because the wider Flanaess is making fewer safe alternatives available. The diocese is therefore richer than it might otherwise be — but also more anxious, because any disruption here now matters to more people and more realms at once.

 

**What this increased traffic means in play:**

*   More toll revenue and more guild profit
*   More political importance for Devarnish at the kingdom level
*   More opportunities for smuggling, bribery, forged manifests, and cargo theft
*   More pressure on Count, Bishop, and guilds to define who truly controls the diocese
*   More urgency whenever eastern disorder threatens the roads or river confidence

#### Rule of the Diocese — Count Westin Mori

**Count Westin Mori**, a Suloise noble aligned with **St. Cuthbert**, has ruled the diocese for six years now, and they have not been happy years. He is a young man in his late twenties, tall, broad-shouldered, sharp in pride, and deeply convinced that his father allowed the diocese to drift into weakness. For ten years before inheriting power he waited impatiently, convinced that he would one day have to undo the mistakes of an older generation: too much liberty for the guildmasters, too much indulgence for Bishop Marquin, and too little real rule from the count’s own seat.

 

Since taking office, Westin has acted accordingly. He revoked many laws favored by his father, cut monies previously granted to several episcopal projects, and tried to reclaim secular initiative in nearly every important matter. These decisions did not make him loved. Rather, they placed him immediately at odds with almost every entrenched power that had grown comfortable under his father’s indifference. Westin is therefore a ruler both active and embattled: he governs with a strong hand because he believes a strong hand is needed, yet every gesture of authority reminds his rivals why they preferred the old arrangement.

 

**What Westin wants:**

*   To restore real comital control over the diocese
*   To reduce guild influence over law, money, and civic custom
*   To prevent Bishop Marquin from turning religious reform into diocesan dominance
*   To keep trade flowing without surrendering secular authority to merchants, clerics, or private interests
*   To prove that Devarnish will be ruled by its Count again, not merely managed by those who profited under his father

#### Bishop Marquin Dorasie — Twenty Years of Moral War

\[

**Bishop Marquin Dorasie** has been Bishop of Devarnish for twenty years and sees the diocese less as a triumph of Velunan trade than as a standing spiritual embarrassment. He believes Devarnish to be the most morally corrupt city in Veluna and has spent two decades fighting what he sees there: vice, luxury, compromise, quiet criminality, and the gradual enthronement of profit over conscience. Under Westin’s father he enjoyed wide practical freedom. Under Count Westin Mori, a ruler of St. Cuthbertan seriousness and strong secular will, he finds that freedom sharply limited.

 

This has led to repeated and increasingly public confrontation between bishop and count. Their quarrels are no longer private matters of policy or letters sealed in wax. They are visible political facts. Westin sees a bishop grown too accustomed to ruling as he pleased. Marquin sees a young count who mistakes appetite for reform and worldliness for strength. Between them lies a city rich enough to tempt everyone and obedient enough to neither.

 

Marquin’s current major project is the formation of a body of **Raoan Knights** in Devarnish, which he believes would drive out undesirable elements and lend real force to moral order. To his supporters, this is a long-overdue correction. To his critics, it is the bishop’s attempt to create a more durable arm of pressure inside an already tense diocese.

* * *

#### Public Conflict Between Count and Bishop

The key truth of Devarnish in 576 CY is that the Count and the Bishop are not merely different in temperament. They are in open contest over what the diocese is for. Under the old count, the bishop effectively ran much as he pleased while secular rule slumbered. Under Westin Mori, that arrangement has ended. The result has been repeated and increasingly public confrontation between a **St. Cuthbert-aligned count** who believes rule must be firm, lawful, and visibly secular, and a long-entrenched bishop who believes Devarnish can only be saved through relentless moral correction.

 

**Their public confrontations generally turn on three questions:**

*   **Who governs trade wealth?** The Count says secular rule must not become hostage to merchants or clerics. The Bishop says wealth without moral oversight rots the soul of the realm.
*   **Who disciplines the city?** Westin’s St. Cuthbertan instincts favor firm order and accountable rule under lawful authority. Marquin believes vice must be rooted out through a sterner religious hand than the Count is willing to permit.
*   **Who speaks for Veluna at the edge?** Westin insists the count’s hand must be seen. Marquin insists the church’s seriousness is what keeps Devarnish worthy at all.

 

No one in the diocese is neutral in this struggle for long. Guilds, captains, customs men, market inspectors, clerks, wealthy houses, barristers, and minor priests all learn quickly which patron’s displeasure they fear most.

* * *

#### Marcus Mori and the Celestial Order

The Count’s representative to the **Celestial Order** is his youngest brother, **Marcus Mori**. The brothers have not always seen eye to eye, yet Marcus remains the closest thing Westin has to a trusted friend. That matters a great deal in a diocese where the Count feels surrounded by guildmasters, clerics, and civic interests that were more comfortable under his father’s softer rule.</p>  

Marcus is therefore more than a family appointment. He is Westin’s most reliable political extension beyond the local struggle, able to speak for the Count in wider Velunan noble politics with a degree of loyalty few others can match. For campaign use, Marcus is valuable precisely because he is not merely a flatterer. A younger brother with imperfect agreement but real trust is often worth more than a hall full of obedient courtiers.

* * *

#### Guildmaster Markei Poston — The High Roads Trading Company

**Guildmaster Markei Poston** is widely considered the most powerful man in Devarnish after the Count and the Bishop. A half-elven master of trade with connections across the Flanaess, he controls the **High Roads Trading Company**, the largest trading concern in Veluna. His contacts, his houses, his factors, and his cargo reach farther than the influence of many small nobles. Markei is exactly what a rich trade diocese eventually produces: a man so deeply woven into the movement of goods that law itself must reckon with him as though he were an institution.

 

Westin has sought ways to rein him in but has not yet found one that would not provoke greater trouble. Markei’s mansion compound on the east side of Devarnish rivals that of the Count himself, and rumors persist that he imagines a future in which comital rule becomes merely ceremonial beside the practical sovereignty of trade. Whether he truly dreams so high matters less than the fact that others can imagine it of him. That is power enough.

* * *

#### Guildmaster Lorit Baskin — The Iron Rings Consortium

**Guildmaster Lorit Baskin**, master of the **Iron Rings Consortium**, is Markei’s chief competitor and one of Count Westin’s few useful allies among the wealthier powers of the diocese. He is far less visible in public life than Markei, leaving daily matters to subordinates and stepping forward only when the matter is large enough to merit his own hand. This makes him harder to read and, to some, more dangerous.

 

Rumor whispers that Lorit had something to do with the old count’s untimely death, though nothing was ever proved. Such rumors cling easily in a city where guild wealth and noble succession touch the same ledgers. Whether true or not, the tale sharpens his reputation. Lorit is the sort of man for whom silence is itself a weapon, and his friendship with Westin must always be read in the light of advantage as well as loyalty.

* * *

#### Jarl — The Preferred Eastern Route

**Jarl** is the great river hinge of the diocese and the preferred eastern route for most traffic leaving Devarnish. It is a large and busy trade town straddling two realms in practical function if not in formal sovereignty, with most of its urban body on the Velunan side of the Velverdyva and additional port infrastructure across the Furyondian bank. A great stone bridge of multiple arches binds the two sides together and allows river vessels to pass beneath. Its docks, warehouses, barges, ropewalks, counting houses, chandlers, ferries, and riverside taverns give it the character of a place that never fully sleeps and never fully cleans itself. :contentReference\[oaicite:6\]{index=6}

 

Because so much eastern traffic prefers Jarl over the woodland crossing, the town has grown in wealth and practical significance. It is where men choose water over risk, records over rumor, and barges over broken axles. It is also where foreigners, smugglers, drovers, riverfolk, customs officers, toll collectors, and opportunists all meet. Jarl is therefore not merely a trade town. It is a sorting place for people as much as for goods.

* * *

#### The Iron Road and the Fallen Timber Tavern

The **Iron Road** runs from Devarnish eastward through the Iron Woods toward Verbobonc, but its condition has become one of the most telling symbols of the diocese’s geography. It is well-kept until the woods are reached. Thereafter, neglect becomes plain: fallen trees, washed-out stretches, and a general sense that the road remains lawful more by memory than by maintenance. Most wise travelers still use it only when need outweighs caution.

 

At its rough midpoint stands the **Fallen Timber Tavern**, widely accepted as the only reliable safe haven on the road after dark. It is a vital landmark — half inn, half fortress of habit, the one place where all who travel the road make common cause in their desire to be indoors before night closes over the trees. This makes the tavern a powerful adventure anchor. Messages can be missed there, caravans can gather there, road gossip can ferment there, and anything hunting the road may naturally circle toward it.

 

**For DM use, the Iron Road is ideal for:**

*   Escort missions between Veluna and Verbobonc
*   Missing couriers and delayed dispatches
*   Bandit or cult ambushes concealed by woodland neglect
*   Tense overnights at the Fallen Timber Tavern
*   Border incidents that never happened “inside” a cleanly agreed jurisdiction

#### Devarnish and the Wider Campaign

The Diocese of Devarnish matters greatly to the wider sandbox because it is where Veluna first feels the practical consequences of eastern disorder. When trade grows uncertain in Verbobonc, Devarnish notices. When river traffic shifts, Devarnish notices. When the Gift of Beauty crisis deepens and missing Velunan clergy demand inquiry, Devarnish is one of the first dioceses likely to assemble routes, escorts, ledgers, and clerical attention for action. It is not simply a location. It is a response point.

 

This is also why the diocese should matter to the players. If they are dealing with road security, diplomatic fallout, clergy on the move, guild manipulation, or east-west travel, then Devarnish is one of the places where consequence will gather fastest. A recovered witness, a damaged convoy, a lost coffer, a forged cargo seal, a bishop’s demand, or a count’s order may all echo more loudly here than in quieter lands deeper in the kingdom.

* * *

#### What the Temple of Elemental Evil Would Exploit Here

A cunning enemy need not assault Devarnish openly. It need only make every living tension in the diocese more difficult. The Temple would benefit enormously from increasing distrust between Count and Bishop, sharpening guild rivalry, spreading fear along the Iron Road, and using Jarl’s busy traffic to hide agents, contraband, or false papers. Devarnish’s strength is movement. Its weakness is that movement requires trust at exactly the points where too many men now stand to profit by its failure.

 

**The most profitable seams to attack would be these:**

*   **Road confidence:** make the Iron Road seem more dangerous still and the river route more corrupt
*   **Guild competition:** turn Markei and Lorit into mutually destructive enemies
*   **Clerical reform:** provoke backlash against the bishop’s severity among merchants and laborers
*   **Border records:** compromise manifests, toll books, or customs clerks
*   **Family distrust:** use Marcus Mori’s political role to deepen suspicion within the comital house

#### DM Notes: Using the Diocese of Devarnish at the Table

**Use Devarnish when thou wantest Veluna to feel rich, contested, and strategically awake.**

 

*   **For trade intrigue:** manifests, cargo fraud, toll disputes, warehouse theft, guild sabotage, river tariffs
*   **For church politics:** Bishop Marquin’s moral campaign, Raoan knights, censures, inquisitive clerics, reform decrees
*   **For noble tension:** Westin Mori’s strong-handed rule, Marcus Mori’s divided loyalty, Trisen Mori at Jarl, family strain
*   **For travel play:** the Jarl river route versus the Iron Road decision is itself a campaign choice
*   **For regional consequence:** eastern disorder, Gift of Beauty follow-up, Velunan investigation, border diplomacy with Verbobonc

##### WHY THE DIOCESE OF DEVARNISH MATTERS FOR YOUR CAMPAIGN

Devarnish is one of the best Velunan dioceses for active campaign play because it binds together several major engines at once:

*   **Trade:** Great Western Road, Velverdyva traffic, Jarl, tolls, manifests, warehouses, workshops
*   **Politics:** Count Westin Mori of St. Cuthbert and Bishop Marquin Dorasie are in open struggle for diocesan control
*   **Guild Power:** Markei Poston and Lorit Baskin make commerce into a rival form of rule
*   **Travel Risk:** the safe river route and the dangerous Iron Road create immediate adventure structure
*   **Regional Pressure:** eastern news, Verbobonc instability, and wider Flanaess traffic all strike here early
*   **Demographics:** A populous and mixed diocese — heavily Oeridian, but with significant Suloise and notable demihuman communities shaped by trade, roads, and river exchange

 

Use Devarnish when you want a Velunan article that does more than describe a pious land. Use it when roads matter, when bishops and counts should glare at one another in public, when guild money should make law tremble, and when a party must decide whether to trust the river, trust the road, or trust no one at all.
