E6 The Riverside Inn
====================

_**A Haven for the Down-and-Out  
**_The Riverside Inn is Ryemend’s cheapest roof—worn, drafty, and perilously easy to get robbed in—yet it sits in the most useful place imaginable: across the cobblestone market square from the other inns, with fenced inner-city farm plots behind, and beyond them the trade river and stone battlement walls that guard the district. Use it as your pressure-cooker “poor door” into Ryemend: where desperation speaks plainly, and danger is paid for in copper.

##### Enter The Riverside Inn

Across the square—where clean lanterns shine upon better doors—the Riverside squats as if it hath weathered one storm too many. Shutters hang awry; the roofline sags in a way that makes thee glance up despite thyself. A hand-painted sign clings to its chain: RIVERSIDE INN, the letters sun-faded and twice touched-up.  
  
Behind it, past a crooked fence, lie narrow plots of inner-city field—ragged rows of cabbage and barley—then the river’s dull gleam, and beyond that the hard, grey rise of the city’s battlements.  
  
When the door opens, a stale warmth crawleth out: old ale, damp wool, and the sour breath of folk who have slept too close together for too long. It is not comfort… but it is shelter.

### What This Place Is

The Riverside Inn is a **longstanding refuge for the down-and-out**, famed for two things: **cheap beds** and **no questions asked**. It is undeniably worn—missing shutters, leaky roof, and scant comforts—but it endures because Ryemend produces folk who cannot pay for gentler places.

It offers:

*   **4 private rooms** on the top floor (still shabby, merely less awful).
*   **Common rooms** on the middle floor, each with a small hearth and shared misery.
*   **No room service**, **no running water**, **no chamber pots**, and blankets that are “often unclean.”

**DM Truth:** This is not where heroes come to be praised. This is where they come to be _seen_—by the desperate, the hungry, the informants, and the predatory.

#### Placement on the Square

Use its position as a visual lesson in Ryemend’s class-lines:

*   **Front:** the broad **market-square cobbles**, with traffic flowing between E4 Season’s End and E5 House of Cards.
*   **Back:** fenced **inner-city farm plots** (watchful tenants, scarecrows, and tools locked up).
*   **Beyond:** the **trade river** and the **stone battlement wall**, a stern horizon that says, _Verbobonc is defended… but not everyone inside is safe._

This makes the Riverside perfect for scenes where the party stands on the square and must decide: **clean comfort, risky vice, or hard necessity**.

#### History: A Longstanding Refuge

The Riverside Inn has served as a refuge for the down-and-out for many years. Its affordable prices and basic accommodations have made it a staple for those in need of shelter.

##### Historical Highlights

*   **Establishment:** Known for its affordability and basic services
*   **Legacy:** Continues to serve the down-and-out, maintaining its reputation as a last resort

_Supporting the Marginalized_

The Riverside Inn plays a crucial role in the community by providing affordable shelter for the down-and-out, poor farmers, and drunks. It fosters a sense of belonging for those who have nowhere else to turn.

##### Key Relationships

*   **Local Poor:** Regularly serves the down-and-out and poor farmers
*   **Travelers:** Provides a last resort for those passing through with limited means
*   **Community Support:** Acts as a crucial support system for the marginalized

### Goods, Services, and Risks

##### Lodging

The Riverside charges **75% of standard prices**—cheap enough to tempt even folk who know better.

**Private Rooms:** 4 (top floor)  
**Common Rooms:** middle floor, small hearths

##### “Last-Resort” Hazards (DM Lever)

*   **Common rooms:** **20% nightly chance** a guest “catches a disease.”
*   **Common rooms:** **30% nightly chance** of being **pickpocketed by a rogue**.

Use these as stakes, not punishment: the party can reduce or negate them by smart choices, watches, friendships, and spending coin elsewhere.

### Player Interaction Hooks

##### Why PCs Come Here

*   They need cheap beds, quick, without scrutiny
*   They want to meet folk who won’t enter Season’s End or Lester’s
*   They’re seeking rumors from riverhands, failed drovers, or frightened couriers
*   They need a “no questions” place to stash someone for one night (with consequences)

##### If They Linger

*   Gwyneth offers a bargain: “Keep the knives down tonight, and I’ll tell thee which men in this house lie for coin.”
*   Dylan quietly asks for help moving a “sleeping drunk” (who is not sleeping, and not drunk)

##### What Goes Wrong

*   A pickpocket lifts the wrong purse—containing a sealed note tied to your Temple plotline
*   A sick guest coughs blood by the hearth, and someone whispers, “Don’t let the Wardens see…”
*   A runner bursts in from the riverward fields: “They’re dragging something from the reeds!”

##### Gwyneth Llewellyn (Owner)

A resilient, no-nonsense woman who runs the Riverside with a firm hand and a hard eye—because mercy without rules becomes slaughter in places like this.

**Wants**

*   Keep the inn open (because folk truly need it)
*   Keep violence out of the common rooms
*   Keep the Wardens from deciding the place is more trouble than it’s worth

**Secret (DM)  
**Gwyneth remembers faces. Not kindly—accurately. If the party seeks anonymity, this is a poor choice.

**Dialogue (Gwyneth)**

*   “Coin first. Trouble last. That is the order of things.”
*   “If thou must sleep with one eye open, do it quiet.”
*   “No knives in the common. If thou canst not obey, take thy pride elsewhere.”

##### Dylan Powel (Caretaker)

Gruff, practical, loyal—he looks like a man carved from old fence-posts and sour weather. He keeps the hearths lit, the worst leaks patched, and the worst men reminded that Gwyneth’s rules still bite.

**Dialogue (Dylan)**

*   “Feet off the bench. That plank’s older than thy manners.”
*   “If I catch thee with another man’s purse, I’ll hang thee by thy belt till dawn.”

##### Rumors / Secrets / Plot Hooks (12)

1.  A riverhand swears a barge came in **light** after riding **deep** all week—“like it threw cargo overboard.”
2.  A drover mutters of “road-wardens” who ask after travelers southbound—too polite to be honest.
3.  A pickpocket has been lifting **only letters**, not coin.
4.  A farmer from the fenced plots claims someone walks the rows at night, leaving **no bootprints** in the mud.
5.  A guest offers to sell a **map** to “safe routes” outside the walls—drawn from hearsay and fear.
6.  Dylan’s patchwork on the roof hides a small crawlspace used to listen in on the common room.
7.  A ragged priest says, “I saw the symbol—fourfold—on a crate by moonlight,” then refuses to say more.
8.  A courier begs the party to deliver a message… then cannot remember who it’s for (only the meeting place: Lester’s).
9.  Gwyneth has barred one man from entry—yet he keeps returning under new hats.
10.  Two guests are not travelers at all, but watchers counting faces on the square.
11.  A sick-bed victim is actually poisoned—someone wants it to look like “Riverside fever.”
12.  The pickpocket is a terrified youth paying “rent” to an unseen handler in the market square.
